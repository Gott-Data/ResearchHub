# CLAUDE.md — Gott Data Research Hub

This file is the editorial constitution for everything published under the Gott Data brand. Read it before drafting any research artifact. If a request to draft something conflicts with what is written here, follow this file and flag the conflict.

---

## 1. What Gott Data is

Gott Data is the research arm of Laxatunga ehf. It is a confidential research firm that answers one strategic question better than anyone else:

> Where will your category be in three years, and where should you be standing when it gets there?

Every public artifact published on the research hub exists to demonstrate the firm's capacity to answer that question. Every artifact must, directly or indirectly, deepen the brand's authority on category foresight.

**What the firm publishes:** methodology, frameworks, points of view on where categories are going, and original public research on categories where the firm has both teachable insight and a demonstrable view.

**What the firm does not publish:** named client work, specific findings from client engagements, content that identifies a client's strategy directly or indirectly, or thought leadership written in the voice of a generic consulting firm.

---

## 2. The brand voice in one paragraph

Gott Data writes the way a senior partner at a serious research firm speaks to a CEO they respect: plainly, declaratively, with confidence earned by having done the work. Short sentences. Anglo-Saxon words where possible. No hedging when conclusions are clear; explicit acknowledgement where they are not. The reading age of the *Financial Times*, never *Harvard Business Review*. No exclamation marks. No emojis. No buzzwords. The reader is one human, treated as an adult.

---

## 3. Voice rules (apply to every artifact)

### Do

- **Lead with the conclusion.** State what you found in the first one or two sentences. The reader can stop reading after that and still come away with the recommendation.
- **Use first-person plural when speaking as the firm** ("we found," "we recommend"). Use second-person singular when addressing the reader ("you," "your category"). Never "organisations like yours."
- **State uncertainty explicitly when it exists.** "We do not know whether X. We do know that Y." Sage brands earn trust by knowing the edge of their own knowledge.
- **Show your reasoning trail.** Where a conclusion is non-obvious, walk the reader through how the lenses produced it. Never assert without giving the path.
- **Use concrete examples and specific numbers** where you have them. "Three of the seven players in the category" beats "many players in the category."
- **End every piece with one of three things:** a recommendation, an open question worth thinking about, or a quiet invitation to engage with the firm (never a sales pitch).

### Don't

- **No exclamation marks.** Anywhere. Ever.
- **No emojis.**
- **No hyperbole or superlatives** unless they are demonstrably true and necessary. "Best-in-class," "game-changing," "revolutionary," "unprecedented" — all banned.
- **No banned words:** *unlock, leverage (as verb), synergy, transform (as a generic claim), empower, holistic (overused — be specific), disruptive (overused), seamless, robust, journey (as in "transformation journey"), ecosystem (unless literally meant), curate, paradigm, deep-dive.* If one of these is the only word that fits, you have not thought hard enough.
- **No question headlines** except where the question is the actual frame of the piece. Headlines should state, not tease.
- **No clickbait, no contrarianism for its own sake.** Strong views, defensible by the research, never strong views for attention.
- **No corporate hedging** ("may potentially," "could possibly," "in some cases"). Either you know or you don't. If you don't, say so directly.
- **No name-dropping clients.** Ever. The confidentiality discipline applies to all public artifacts without exception.
- **No emojis.** Worth repeating.

## 4. The five voice qualities, with examples

### 4.1 Plain

> ❌ Organisations operating in the contemporary commercial landscape are facing an increasingly complex set of challenges related to the operationalisation of artificial intelligence capabilities.
>
> ✅ Most companies are struggling to put AI into production. The reasons are not technical.

### 4.2 Declarative

> ❌ It could be argued that the category may be subject to consolidation pressures over the medium term.
>
> ✅ This category will consolidate by 2028. Three of the seven players will be acquired or fold.

### 4.3 Restrained

> ❌ We're thrilled to share game-changing insights from our revolutionary research methodology that empowers leading organisations to unlock unprecedented foresight!
>
> ✅ We did four-lens research on a category question. The findings are below.

### 4.4 Direct

> ❌ Organisations like yours may wish to consider the implications of these findings for their strategic planning processes.
>
> ✅ If you run a mid-market player in this category, here is what we'd do.

### 4.5 Quietly confident

> ❌ While we cannot claim to have all the answers, we hope our humble contribution to the discourse may prove useful.
>
> ✅ This is what we found. The reasoning is below. Reasonable people may disagree on point three.

## 5. The Four-Lens Method

Every piece of original Gott Data research, whether public or private, is structured around four lenses. The discipline is to apply all four to any category question. The lenses are not optional. They are not interchangeable. A piece that examines a question through fewer than four lenses is not Gott Data research.

### Lens 1 — Data
What the numbers say. Quantitative evidence: market data, financial data, public filings, structured extraction from public digital surfaces, internal data where available. The Data lens answers: *what is the measurable shape of the category, and what direction are the measurable variables moving?*

### Lens 2 — Human
What people are actually doing. Behavioural evidence: customer behaviour, employee behaviour, decision-maker patterns, ethnographic observation, individual-level profiling where the question demands resolution. The Human lens answers: *how do the people in this category actually behave and decide, and what is changing in their behaviour that the data does not yet show?*

### Lens 3 — Technology
What is becoming possible, cheap, or obsolete. The technical substrate of the category: AI capabilities, infrastructure shifts, platform dynamics, adjacent technology trajectories. The Technology lens answers: *what will the technical substrate of this category look like in three years, and which assumptions about the current stack are about to break?*

### Lens 4 — Category
The structural forces acting on the market itself: regulation, capital flows, talent movement, consolidation, new entrants, distribution shifts. The Category lens answers: *what is happening to the shape of the market that no single player can see from inside it?*

### How the lenses appear in writing

In public artifacts, the four lenses can either be made explicit (named section headings) or used invisibly (the structure underneath a piece that flows as a single narrative). Both are valid. The named-lens structure is best for whitepapers, methodology pieces, and full research reports. The invisible structure is better for essays, field notes, and shorter opinion pieces.

Whether visible or not, **a Gott Data artifact never leaves a lens unexamined.** If a piece is silent on the human lens or the technology lens, the question being asked was incomplete, and the piece should be reframed.

## 6. Artifact types and their conventions

The research hub publishes several distinct artifact types. Each has a specific shape, length, and purpose. Do not mix conventions across types.

### 6.1 Flagship Research Piece

**Purpose:** The cornerstone artifact of the research hub. One major public piece per quarter. Each is the highest-effort, highest-authority artifact the firm publishes.

**Length:** 4,000–7,000 words. Long enough to be substantive, short enough to be read in one sitting by a CEO on a flight.

**Structure:**
1. **Opening hook** — one paragraph stating the category, the question, and the headline finding. The reader knows the punchline by the end of paragraph one.
2. **Why this question matters now** — 200–400 words placing the question in the present moment.
3. **What existing research says (and where it falls short)** — a short, fair summary of the syndicated and public views, with the specific gaps Gott Data's work fills.
4. **Findings through each lens** — four named sections, one per lens. Each section is 600–1,200 words. Each makes specific, defensible claims, with reasoning.
5. **Synthesis** — what the four lenses together imply. This is the part of the piece that creates competitive advantage for the reader.
6. **Recommendations** — what we would do if we were running a player in this category. Specific. Practical. Sized by player type (incumbent, challenger, new entrant) if relevant.
7. **What we considered and rejected** — at least two alternative interpretations the research weighed and discarded, with reasons.
8. **What we are uncertain about** — explicit. The reader sees the edge of the firm's knowledge.
9. **Method and sources** — a brief appendix on how the research was conducted, what sources were used, what was excluded.

**Tone:** Authoritative, rigorous, opinionated. This is the firm's most important public artifact. It should read like the kind of memo a senior partner would give a board.

### 6.2 Whitepaper

**Purpose:** A focused, evidence-heavy treatment of a specific question within a category. Less editorial than the flagship piece; more like a primary research document.

**Length:** 2,500–5,000 words.

**Structure:**
1. Executive summary (one page maximum, includes the headline finding and the recommendation)
2. The question, stated clearly
3. Research approach (which lenses, what sources, what methods)
4. Findings, organised by lens or by theme
5. Implications for different player types
6. Recommendation
7. Limitations and what further research would clarify
8. Sources appendix

**Tone:** Slightly more formal than the flagship piece. The reader is expected to be working through it deliberately, possibly with a pen.

### 6.3 Brief

**Purpose:** A short, punchy piece that takes a single observation or finding and develops it concisely. Briefs are the most frequent artifact on the hub.

**Length:** 800–1,500 words.

**Structure:**
1. **The observation** — one paragraph, lead with the conclusion.
2. **Why it matters** — two or three paragraphs of context and stakes.
3. **What the evidence says** — the data, the behaviour, the technology, the category dynamics. The lenses are used invisibly; do not name them as sections in a brief.
4. **What this means for you** — practical implications.
5. **A closing line that lands** — one sentence that crystallises the takeaway.

**Tone:** Tight, opinionated, confident. A brief that needs hedging language is not finished.

### 6.4 Methodology piece

**Purpose:** Explains how the firm thinks and works. The Four-Lens Method itself is the canonical example, but methodology pieces also include how the firm approaches specific kinds of questions (e.g., "How we research three-year category foresight," "How we anonymise confidential work in public writing").

**Length:** 1,200–3,000 words.

**Structure:**
1. The problem the method solves (what is broken about the current way of doing this)
2. The principle behind the method
3. The method itself, in steps or components
4. An anonymised example of the method applied
5. The method's limits — what it does not do

**Tone:** Teaching tone, but not academic. The reader should finish the piece able to either apply the method themselves or understand why hiring Gott Data to apply it is the better option. Both outcomes serve the brand.

### 6.5 Framework

**Purpose:** A reusable mental model the firm has developed for thinking about a recurring problem. Frameworks are simpler than methodologies — they are the conceptual scaffolds, not the full process.

**Length:** 600–1,500 words.

**Structure:**
1. The problem the framework addresses
2. The framework itself (often visual — a 2x2, a quadrant, a layered model)
3. Each component or quadrant, explained briefly
4. How to use it
5. Where it breaks down

**Tone:** Practical, illustrative. Frameworks should be memorable. If the reader cannot describe the framework to a colleague after one reading, it is too complex.

### 6.6 Essay / Field note

**Purpose:** Shorter, more opinionated pieces. The firm's voice in conversation with what is happening in the world. Field notes are the connective tissue between major artifacts and keep the hub feeling alive.

**Length:** 800–2,000 words.

**Structure:** Loose. An essay does not need rigid sections. It does need a clear argument, a defensible position, and a closing line that earns the time the reader spent.

**Tone:** More personal than other artifacts. An essay can be signed by an individual within the firm; flagship research and whitepapers are signed by the firm itself.

### 6.7 Shadow case study

**Purpose:** Demonstrates the shape of confidential work without revealing the client. Lives on the Selected Work page, not in the main research stream, but follows the same voice and discipline.

**Length:** 300–600 words per study.

**Structure (mandatory):**
1. **The Question** — what the client was trying to answer
2. **Why Existing Research Couldn't Answer It** — the gap
3. **What We Did** — the engagement shape, lens by lens (no client-identifying detail)
4. **What We Delivered** — the artefact
5. **What Changed** — where appropriate; left unanswered where not

**Client anonymisation rules:**
- Refer to client only by category and geography ("a European luxury automotive brand," "a Nordic mid-market bank")
- Never name a specific market position, revenue band, or product that could narrow identification
- If in doubt, anonymise more
- A shadow case study must be approved by the client before publication if any chance of identification exists

## 7. Title conventions

Titles for Gott Data artifacts follow specific patterns. They are not headlines in the journalistic sense. They are signals of what the artifact contains.

### Flagship research

> **[Category] in [Year]: Four Lenses on a Market Being Rewritten**

Examples: *European Luxury Automotive in 2029: Four Lenses on a Market Being Rewritten*; *Nordic Banking in 2029: Four Lenses on a Market Being Rewritten*.

### Whitepapers

> **How [specific phenomenon] is reshaping [category]**

Or:

> **The [specific question]: A Gott Data whitepaper**

### Briefs

> **[Concrete claim, stated plainly]**

Examples: *Three Things About the AI Strategy Consultancies That Don't Add Up*; *The Quiet Death of the Annual Forecast*.

### Methodology pieces

> **How we [do specific thing]**

Examples: *How we research three-year category foresight*; *How we apply the Four-Lens Method to acquisition decisions*.

### Frameworks

> **The [name] Framework: [one-line description]**

Example: *The Four-Lens Framework: Holding data, human, technology, and category in one room.*

### General rules

- Never use questions as titles unless the artifact is structured around answering that specific question
- Never use clickbait formulations ("You won't believe what we found about...")
- Never use generic AI-era phrases ("Navigating the AI revolution," "The future of X")
- Capitalise category and proper nouns; otherwise sentence case
- No subtitles longer than one line

## 8. Visual and formatting conventions

The research hub should look like a serious publication, not a marketing site.

### Typography
- Display font: a contemporary serif (the visual identity uses this consistently across the Gott family)
- Body font: a neutral grotesque
- Generous line height (1.6–1.8 for body)
- Generous margins; reading column no wider than 70 characters
- No justified text; left-aligned only

### Formatting inside artifacts
- Use H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections. Avoid H4 and deeper.
- Pull quotes are allowed for memorable lines but used sparingly — maximum one per 1,500 words
- Bullet lists are used when content is genuinely list-shaped. Prose is preferred where possible.
- Numbered lists are used only for sequential steps or rankings
- Bold is used to mark genuinely important phrases within prose, not for emphasis decoration
- Italics for titles of publications, foreign terms, and (rarely) emphasis
- Tables are allowed where comparative data is involved
- Charts and visuals are encouraged where they add information, never where they decorate

### Code and data
- Where data appears in a research piece, it includes its source inline
- Where structured data extraction or analysis informs a piece, the method is described in a brief appendix
- Charts are clean, minimal, and labeled — Edward Tufte aesthetic

### Length discipline
- Every artifact has a target length range (see section 6)
- An artifact that runs significantly over its target length is probably trying to be two artifacts. Split it.
- An artifact that runs significantly under its target length is probably underdeveloped. Deepen it.

## 9. The publishing rhythm

The research hub is most effective when it has a predictable rhythm. The target rhythm:

- **One flagship research piece per quarter** (4 per year)
- **One whitepaper per quarter** (4 per year)
- **One brief per month** (12 per year)
- **One methodology or framework piece per quarter** (4 per year)
- **One essay or field note per month** (12 per year)

That is roughly 36 artifacts per year. The firm should never push out of rhythm to hit a number; an empty week is better than a thin artifact.

## 10. The publication checklist

Before any artifact is published, it must clear this checklist:

- [ ] The artifact has a single clear argument or finding, stated up front
- [ ] All four lenses have been applied (visibly or invisibly)
- [ ] No client is identifiable, directly or indirectly
- [ ] No banned words appear (see section 3)
- [ ] No exclamation marks
- [ ] No emojis
- [ ] No hedging language where the finding is clear
- [ ] Uncertainty is acknowledged where it exists
- [ ] At least one section explicitly addresses what we considered and rejected (for flagship pieces and whitepapers)
- [ ] Sources are cited or method is explained where relevant
- [ ] Title follows the convention for the artifact type
- [ ] Length sits within the target range for the artifact type
- [ ] The closing line earns the reader's time
- [ ] The piece would be defensible if read out loud at a board meeting
- [ ] The piece reads like Gott Data, not like a generic consultancy

If any item fails, the artifact is not ready.

---

## 11. Signing and attribution

- **Flagship research and whitepapers** are signed *Gott Data*, with the named lead partner appearing in the byline as the responsible researcher
- **Briefs and methodology pieces** are signed *Gott Data*
- **Essays and field notes** may be signed by an individual within the firm
- **Shadow case studies** are signed *Gott Data* with no individual attribution

Every artifact carries the publication date and a clear "last updated" date if revised. Substantive revisions trigger a versioned re-publication, not a silent edit.

---

## 12. The closing principle

Everything published under Gott Data competes against everything else a CEO could be reading. The bar is not "is this good marketing content." The bar is "would the senior partner of a serious research firm publish this with their name on it."

If the answer is yes, publish.

If the answer is anything else, do not.
