Most strategic research fails the same way. It answers a question through one lens and calls the answer complete. The data team finds the numbers. The customer team finds the behaviour. The technology team finds the capability. The strategy team finds the structural force. Each team is right about its own slice and wrong about the whole.

The Four-Lens Framework is what we use to keep that from happening. It is the discipline of asking every category question through four lenses — data, human, technology, and category — and refusing to publish until all four have been answered.

## The problem

Category foresight questions, the kind that ask where a market will be in three years, are not solvable through one kind of evidence. The data tells you what is measurable now. It does not tell you what people are about to start doing that the numbers will pick up in six quarters. Behaviour tells you that. Behaviour does not tell you which substrate is about to make those new behaviours cheap. Technology tells you that. Technology does not tell you which of those new behaviours regulators will allow, which incumbents will resist, which distribution channels will close. The category tells you that.

Research that uses one lens produces a confident answer to a smaller question than the one the reader asked. A board that acts on a one-lens answer is making a category decision on partial evidence, and often does not know it.

## The framework

Four lenses. Each one is a different kind of evidence. Each one answers a question the other three cannot. The discipline is that all four are applied to any category question before any answer is published.

## The components

**Data.** What the numbers say. Market data, financial filings, structured extraction from public digital surfaces, internal data where available. The data lens answers: what is the measurable shape of the category, and which measurable variables are moving.

**Human.** What people are actually doing. Customer behaviour, employee behaviour, decision-maker patterns, ethnographic observation, individual-level profiling where the question demands resolution. The human lens answers: how do the people in this category actually behave and decide, and what is changing in their behaviour that the data does not yet show.

**Technology.** What is becoming possible, cheap, or obsolete. AI capabilities, infrastructure shifts, platform dynamics, adjacent technology trajectories. The technology lens answers: what will the technical substrate of this category look like in three years, and which assumptions about the current stack are about to break.

**Category.** The structural forces acting on the market itself. Regulation, capital flows, talent movement, consolidation, new entrants, distribution shifts. The category lens answers: what is happening to the shape of the market that no single player can see from inside it.

The four are not interchangeable. A question answered through three lenses is not three-quarters of a Gott Data answer. It is a Gott Data answer with a known blind spot.

## How to use it

The framework is procedural. Once a category question is framed, we ask it four times — once through each lens — before we synthesise. The order does not matter. The completeness does.

In practice this means four parallel research workstreams against the same question, each with its own evidence base and its own analyst. They do not coordinate during the work. They coordinate at synthesis. The reason is simple: pre-coordinated lenses tend to converge on a comfortable joint answer, and the value of the framework is in the friction between them.

The friction is where the real finding lives. When the data says one thing and the behaviour says a contradictory thing, the contradiction is the signal. Three of the most useful pieces of research we have produced began with a data-versus-human contradiction that none of the four analysts could resolve from inside their own lens.

We synthesise by putting the four lens reports in front of one researcher, whose job is not to write a fifth perspective but to find the shape that explains all four. If a shape can be found, it is usually a forward-looking one: the lens that points furthest ahead anchors the timing. If no shape can be found, the question is probably wrong, and we go back to reframing.

## Where it breaks down

The framework has three honest limits.

First, it is heavy. Four parallel workstreams cost more than a single-lens project. For tactical questions — should we run this promotion, should we hire this person — four lenses is overkill. The framework is built for category foresight, not operational decisions.

Second, the lenses can be poorly sourced. A weak data lens dragged into synthesis pollutes the whole answer. We have learned to be explicit about the strength of evidence in each lens before synthesis begins, and to discount thin lenses rather than treat all four as equal.

Third, the framework assumes the four lenses are sufficient. In some categories they are not. Geopolitical exposure, for instance, sometimes deserves a fifth lens of its own rather than being folded into category dynamics. We add lenses sparingly when the question demands it. We do not subtract them.

## A closing thought

A category question deserves more than one kind of evidence. The Four-Lens Framework is our answer to that. If you are running a player in a category whose three-year shape you cannot describe in one sentence, run your strategy question through the four lenses — even informally — before your next planning cycle. The contradictions you find will be the most useful part of the exercise.
